# House Drums — Classic Four-on-the-Floor Programming

## Quick Reference
- **Tempo**: 120-128 BPM (124 is the sweet spot for classic house)
- **Time Signature**: 4/4
- **Feel**: Driving but groovy, steady pulse with syncopated percussion on top
- **Sound palette**: TR-909 is the gold standard — punchy kick, crispy hats, snappy clap

## Drum Elements

### 1. Kick — The Foundation
- Four-on-the-floor: kick on every beat (1, 2, 3, 4) — this is non-negotiable
- Use a punchy 909-style kick with mid-range definition and low-end weight
- Keep the kick sample short to medium length — a long decay muddies the low end
- Add gentle tape saturation or Drum Buss drive for warmth
- The kick is the loudest element in the mix — everything else sits below it

### 2. Clap/Snare — The Backbeat
- Place a clap on beats 2 and 4
- Layer a snare underneath the clap at -6 to -8 dB for thickness and body
- The clap provides the crack, the snare provides the weight
- Keep both samples short and tight — no long reverb tails on the dry sound
- Send to a reverb return (short room or plate, 0.8-1.2s decay) at low levels for space

### 3. Open Hi-Hat — The Signature
- Open hats on every upbeat (the "and" of each beat) — this IS the house sound
- Use a 909 open hat with medium-short decay so it breathes but doesn't wash out
- Velocity: consistent at 90-110 for a driving, insistent feel
- Set up a mute group so the closed hat chokes the open hat naturally
- This pattern creates the constant push-pull energy that makes house music move

### 4. Closed Hi-Hat — The Groove Layer
- Program 16th notes across the full bar
- Alternate velocities for groove: loud on downbeats (100-110), soft on upbeats (60-75)
- For extra shuffle, drop the velocity on the "e" positions (2nd and 4th 16th of each beat) to 40-55
- Use a tight, crispy closed hat — short decay, no ring
- The closed hat sits behind the open hat in volume — it fills the space, not dominates it

### 5. Percussion Layers
- **Congas/Bongos**: Syncopated hits between kicks, velocity 70-90 — adds Latin swing and warmth
- **Shaker**: 16th notes layered quietly under hats for texture, keep below -10 dB relative to hats
- **Tambourine**: On 8th notes for shimmer in build sections, pan slightly off-center
- **Ride cymbal**: Steady 8th or quarter notes for driving peak-time sections, replaces or supplements open hats
- **Rimshot/Clave**: Sparse syncopated accents for funk — one or two hits per bar maximum
- Tune percussion elements relative to each other so they work as a cohesive kit
- **Loop chopping for unique hits**: Grab a single hit or short snippet from a longer percussion loop (Ctrl+E to split in Ableton), then audition it on different 16th-note positions to find where it best locks into your groove — a single well-placed chop can define the whole feel
- **Two-bar variation**: Double your pattern to 2 bars and shift percussion hits (bongos, congas) to slightly different positions in the second bar — this breaks repetition and plays with listener expectation. Try removing a hit entirely where the listener anticipates it; the silence creates tension

The essential house beat. Four kicks, claps on 2 and 4, open hats on every upbeat. This alone fills a dance floor.

Full 16th-note closed hats with a ride on 8th notes for relentless drive. Conga hits on syncopated offbeats add percussive funk. Use this for peak-time energy and drops.

## Processing

### Drum Bus Treatment
- **Parallel compression**: Send all drums to a return track with a compressor — fast attack (1-2ms), fast release (20-40ms), high ratio (8:1+), threshold pulled way down. Blend the crushed signal at 25-40% alongside dry drums for density without killing transients
- **Transient shaping**: Use Drum Buss or a transient shaper on the drum group — boost attack slightly (+2-3 dB) and reduce sustain to keep hits tight and punchy
- **EQ on drum bus**: Gentle high-shelf boost at 8-10kHz for air, slight cut at 300-400 Hz to reduce boxiness

### Per-Element Tips
- Kick: light compression (2:1) to even out dynamics, sidechain the bass to it
- Clap/Snare: boost 1-2kHz for snap, cut below 200 Hz to stay out of kick's range
- Hats: high-pass at 400-500 Hz, slight stereo width on closed hats only

## Swing & Groove
- Apply swing to hi-hats and percussion only — kick and clap stay locked to the grid
- **Swing amount**: 54-58% (where 50% is straight) for classic house groove
- Alternatively, use Ableton's groove pool: load a MPC or 909 groove template at 30-50% intensity
- For deeper feels, nudge the clap 5-15ms ahead of the beat for a pushing, urgent energy
- Velocity variation on hats matters more than timing variation — randomize velocity +/- 10% for life
- **Slot the bass into the groove**: When programming bass alongside drums, place bass notes on offbeat 8th and 16th notes where the kick is NOT hitting — at most one bass note on a downbeat per bar. This interlocks bass and kick rhythmically, adds funk through syncopation, and avoids low-end frequency collision without needing heavy sidechain compression

## Build Order
1. Program the kick: four-on-the-floor, every beat
2. Add the clap on beats 2 and 4, layer a snare underneath for body
3. Place open hats on every upbeat — verify the mute group chokes correctly
4. Fill in closed hats on 16th notes with alternating velocity
5. Apply swing (54-58%) to hats and percussion, keep kick and clap on-grid
6. Add one percussion element (conga or shaker) for movement
7. Double to 2 bars — shift percussion hits in bar 2 for variation, remove or move a hit to play against expectation
8. Set up parallel compression on the drum bus return
9. Add transient shaping to tighten the overall kit
10. Mix: kick loudest, clap next, open hat behind, everything else tucked under
