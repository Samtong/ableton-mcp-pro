# Drum Swing & Groove

## The Golden Rule of Swing
**Strong beats stay on time. Weak beats arrive late.**

## MPC-Style Swing Percentages
Swing works by delaying the even-numbered 16th notes (2, 4, 6, 8, etc.) within each beat. The percentage controls how far they shift:

- **50%** — Dead straight, no swing. Both 16th notes evenly spaced.
- **54%** — Subtle looseness. The beat breathes without sounding "swung." Good starting point for modern trap and boom bap.
- **58%** — Noticeable groove. A sweet spot for laid-back hip-hop — loose enough to feel human, tight enough to stay locked.
- **62%** — Strong swing. Works well at 85-95 BPM for that head-nod bounce. At 90 BPM this feels looser than a mathematically perfect triplet swing.
- **66%** — Perfect triplet swing. The second 16th note lands exactly on an 8th-note triplet. Classic jazz and soul shuffle territory.
- **70-75%** — Exaggerated swing. The second note is drastically late. Use sparingly — works for lo-fi or intentionally sloppy feels.

**Pro tip**: Apply different swing amounts per element. Swing hi-hats at 58-62%, snare at 52-54%, and leave the kick on the grid. This layered approach is how producers like J Dilla and Madlib build complex grooves from simple patterns.

## Beat Strength Hierarchy
1. **Strongest**: On the beat (1, 2, 3, 4)
2. **Weaker**: On the "and" (8th note offbeats)
3. **Weakest**: On the "e" or "uh" (16th note positions)

## How to Apply Swing

### Kick Placement
- Keep the downbeat kick (beat 1) perfectly on time
- Kicks on weak beats (ands, e's, uh's): nudge slightly to the RIGHT (late)
- Lower velocity on kicks that land on weak beats
- Result: main kicks anchor the groove, ghost kicks add laid-back feel

### Snare Placement
- Main snare on 2 and 4: can offset slightly from other elements on the same beat
- Additional snares: ONLY place on weakest spots (e and uh of beats)
- Avoid putting extra snares directly on kicks — keeps it less cluttered
- Lower velocity significantly on added snares vs. main snare hits
- Make it obvious which snares are the "main" ones (2 and 4) vs. decorative

### Hi-Hats
- Draw on 8th notes (every "and")
- Lower velocity on offbeat hi-hats (the "and" hits)
- Easy method: set first hi-hat strong, second weak, select both, Cmd+D across the loop
- Nudge all hi-hats slightly late for extra swing
- Line up any added kicks with the hi-hat timing above them

### Rim/Percussion
- Can push slightly EARLY for contrast against late kicks
- Creates push-pull tension in the groove

### Shuffle vs. Swing
These terms are often confused but describe different feels:
- **Shuffle** is triplet-based — the middle note of each triplet group is removed, creating a long-short pattern. Think classic blues, jazz, and house music. Mathematically precise at 66%.
- **Swing** is looser — notes shift off the grid by varying amounts without strict triplet adherence. The "Dilla feel" lives here: notes land between grid lines in ways that defy simple fractions.
- In Ableton, shuffle is what you get from the Groove Pool's swing amount at 66%. True swing comes from manual nudging or extracting grooves from audio.

### Behind the Beat vs. Ahead of the Beat
Where your notes sit relative to the grid changes the entire character:
- **Behind the beat** (notes arrive late): Laid-back, heavy, hypnotic. Classic for boom bap, lo-fi, and neo-soul. Nudge elements 5-20ms late. J Dilla's "drunken" feel comes from hi-hats and snares sitting behind the beat at different amounts.
- **Ahead of the beat** (notes arrive early): Urgent, driving, anxious. Used in punk, some techno, and uptempo funk. Nudge elements 5-10ms early.
- **On the beat**: Clean, precise, mechanical. Works for EDM and pop but can feel lifeless for hip-hop.
- **Mix and match** for tension: kick on the grid, snare 10ms late, hi-hats 15ms late, rim click 5ms early. The disagreement between elements is what makes a groove feel alive.

## Energy Sections (I → A → B → O)

### 3 Changes Per Section
When moving from one energy level to the next, make exactly 3 obvious changes:

**I (Intro) → A:**
1. Add consistent hi-hats (8th notes with velocity variation)
2. Add clap on 2 and 4
3. Add a small percussion hit before claps

**A → B (max energy):**
1. Make kick more active (add kicks on ands, keep them late + quiet)
2. Replace simple percussion with ride (gate mode, vary lengths)
3. Add toms or extra percussion fills

**B → O (Outro):**
- Strip elements back
- Remove the busy kicks
- Remove rides and toms
- Keep minimal kick (just downbeats) + some hi-hat
- Distinct from Intro but lower energy

## Variation Within Loops
- Duplicate loop to make it 2 bars
- Change ONE small thing in the second half (move a kick, add/remove a hit)
- Minimal effort, doubles the time before listener gets bored

## Velocity Curves & Humanization
- **Natural velocity pattern for hi-hats**: Vary from light (40-60) to medium (70-90) continuously and somewhat randomly — avoid strict alternation, which sounds robotic
- **Humanization random ranges**: Add +/- 5-15 velocity randomization to hi-hats and percussion. Keep kick and snare randomization tighter (+/- 3-8) so the backbone stays solid
- **Accenting the offbeat**: Try making the swung (late) note louder than the on-beat note — this flips the expected emphasis and creates a rolling, hypnotic groove common in neo-soul and lo-fi
- **Velocity curves by element**: Kick 100-120, main snare 100-127, ghost snare 20-50, hi-hats 40-100 (wide range), percussion 50-80

## Key Principles
- Changes between sections must be **immediately obvious** and **present throughout the loop**
- Don't overdo it — busy ≠ groove
- Velocity is your best friend for creating feel
- Ghost notes (low velocity) create depth without clutter
