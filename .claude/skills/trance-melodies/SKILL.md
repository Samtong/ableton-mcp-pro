# Trance Melodies

## Quick Reference
- **Scale**: Major keys work well (C major is common)
- **Rule**: Less is more — 3-4 notes max for catchy melodies
- **Key idea**: Trance is the intersection of melody AND rhythm

## Build Order
1. Chord progression first (the foundation)
2. Lead melody on top
3. Chord melody (pad layer) for depth

## 1. Chord Progression (foundation)
- Start with a simple major progression
- Example in C major: C major -> A minor -> E minor
- This provides the harmonic foundation that everything else plays against
- Use an offbeat bass alongside (standard trance bassline)

### Tension and Resolution with Suspended and 7th Chords
- Replace a standard triad with a **sus4** (e.g., Csus4 = C, F, G) to create unresolved tension, then resolve it to the major chord on the next beat or bar
- Add **7th intervals** to chords (e.g., Am7, Cmaj7) for emotional depth — the 7th wants to resolve downward, pulling the listener forward
- Use a **ii-V-I pattern** (e.g., Dm -> G -> C) for classic trance tension-release, or substitute an unexpected chord like the vi in place of the I for a deceptive cadence that delays satisfaction
- Avoid landing on the tonic note in your melody during buildup sections — use the 3rd or 5th instead to keep tension alive until the drop

## 2. Lead Melody

### Note Selection
- Pick **no more than 3-4 notes** from the scale
- Fewer notes = catchier melody (less information for the brain to process)
- Example in C major: use B (7th), F (4th), G (5th), E (3rd), and B octave up

### Rhythm First Technique
- Write the rhythm before the melody: pick ONE note and create a rhythmic pattern
- Once the rhythm feels good, duplicate and change notes
- The rhythm is as important as the note choice for catchiness

### Octave Trick
- Use octave jumps to create variety while keeping it simple
- You're still using only 3 notes (e.g., C, E, B) but the octave jump gives a different feel
- Octaves are your best friend for adding energy without complexity

### Call and Response Patterns
- Split the melody into two halves: a **question phrase** (call) and an **answer phrase** (response) — the call introduces tension, the response resolves it
- Differentiate call from response using **timbre** (bright supersaw vs. softer pluck) or **pitch register** (high call / low response, or vice versa)
- Rhythmic contrast works too: a syncopated call followed by a straighter, on-the-beat response
- This technique gives structure to a melody without complex theory — just two complementary phrases trading back and forth

## 3. Chord Melody (depth layer)
- A pad layer that does NOT follow the main chord progression
- Uses notes from the same scale but plays different chords
- Example: when the main chords change to A minor, this pad still holds the first chord
- This creates rich harmonic interaction and depth
- Easy to do: just stay in the scale (use Ableton's Scale MIDI effect if needed)

### Why Chord Melodies Matter
- Adds depth to the chord progression
- Gives arrangement flexibility — when the lead has been playing too long, you can feature the chord melody instead
- Creates layers that can be introduced/removed for builds and breakdowns

## Production Notes
- Don't overthink it — start simple, add complexity only if needed
- The interaction between chord progression and melody is what makes it work; sound design is secondary to good note and rhythm choices

### Building Energy Through Note Density
- Start with **longer, sustained notes** (half/quarter notes) for space, then increase density to **8th, 16th, and 32nd-note runs** into the drop
- Progressively **move up the scale** through bars — set the final notes higher than anything preceding for an uplifting lift
- Mix short stabby notes with sustained ones to create rhythmic interest within a phrase

### Delay and Arpeggiator Techniques
- A **ping-pong delay** (synced to 1/8 or dotted 1/8) fills space and creates rhythmic movement from simple note input
- Use the synth's **arpeggiator** to sketch ideas — arpeggiate chord tones and listen for patterns that suggest a melody; layer an arpeggiated pluck under the lead for shimmer
- Automate delay feedback or wet/dry mix during builds for an intensifying effect

### Sound Design for Trance Leads
- **Supersaw leads**: layer 5-7 detuned saw oscillators, spread stereo width wide, low-pass filter with moderate resonance for warmth
- **Pluck sounds**: short attack, fast decay, no sustain — great for arpeggiated lines and response phrases
- Layer different synth qualities (crisp on top, warm underneath, wide in stereo) and add **modwheel automation** and subtle **pitch bend** for expressive timbral movement
